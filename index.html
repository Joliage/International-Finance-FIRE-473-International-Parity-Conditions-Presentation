Are exchange-rate changes predictable? The parity conditions are the core financial theory that links three things together: prices, interest rates, and exchange rates.

6.1
Section One
Prices & Exchange Rates
If the same thing sells in two markets with no barriers, it should cost the same everywhere. From that one rule we get the Law of One Price, Purchasing Power Parity, and the famous Big Mac Index.
6.1 · The Foundation

The Law of One Price

If identical goods can be sold in two markets and there are no transport costs or trade barriers, the price should be identical in both markets — once you convert currencies.
Why it almost never holds perfectly
  • Goods do not move between countries at zero cost.
  • Many services are not tradable at all (think haircuts).
  • Quality and tastes differ across countries.
  • Taxes, rents, and regulation distort local prices.
The two assumptions
1 · Free trade
No tariffs, quotas, or shipping frictions.
2 · Perfect substitutes
The product is truly identical across markets.
Relax these and the theory still guides the long-run trend — just not today's price.

6.1 · The Math

From prices to an exchange rate

Knowing a product's price in two currencies lets you back out the exchange rate the market should set.
P$ × S¥ = P¥The dollar price times the exchange rate (yen per dollar) equals the yen price.
S¥/$ = P¥ ÷ P$Rearranged: the implied exchange rate is just the ratio of the two local prices.
Worked example
A basket of goods costs ¥1,000 in Japan and $10 in the United States.
S = ¥1,000 ÷ $10 = ¥100 = $1.00
A wider version replaces a single product with a whole price index — that gives Absolute Purchasing Power Parity, coming up next.

6.1 · Purchasing Power Parity

Absolute PPP: one basket, one rate

Swap a single product for a whole basket of goods (a price index) and the same logic gives the "real" exchange rate.
The absolute version
S = PI¥ ÷ PI$The spot rate equals the ratio of the two countries' price indices. It states what the rate is today.
"The spot exchange rate is determined by the relative prices of similar baskets of goods."
Two honest conclusions from the data
  • PPP holds up well over the very long run, poorly over short periods.
  • It works better for countries with high inflation and underdeveloped capital markets.
Global Finance in Practice: an Uber ride costs wildly different amounts in Mumbai, Singapore, and Kuala Lumpur. Regulated fares and local wages break parity — proof that price controls distort the law of one price.
FIRE 473 · International FinanceCH 6 · 6.1 PRICES & EXCHANGE RATES
6.1 · A Famous Yardstick

The Big Mac Index

The Economist has tracked it since 1986. A Big Mac is a near-perfect law-of-one-price product — so its price compares currencies in plain sight.
Why a burger works
  • Nearly identical everywhere — same recipe, process, and brand standard.
  • Built from local ingredients, labor, and rent — so its price reflects domestic costs, not imports.
The three-step calculation
  • 1. Big Mac price in dollars = local price ÷ actual rate.
  • 2. Implied PPP rate = local price ÷ U.S. price.
  • 3. Over / under-valuation = (implied − actual) ÷ actual.
Read it carefully: a big "undervaluation" signals where a currency may head over the long term — not what it's worth today.

6.1 · Reality Check

Strengths and limits

What makes it good
  • The product is genuinely consistent across markets.
  • Priced from local inputs, so it reflects domestic cost levels.
  • Simple, vivid, and globally recognized.
Where it breaks
  • Big Macs can't be traded across borders.
  • Local rents, taxes, and wages distort the price.
  • It signals the long run, not today's fair value.
The same idea, other products
Investec built an African Beer Standard using a 375 ml lager — because McDonald's hadn't reached most of the continent. Any homogeneous, locally produced good can serve as a PPP yardstick.

6.1 · The Practical Version

Relative PPP: inflation moves the rate

Absolute PPP rarely nails today's rate. The relative version is more useful: it predicts the change in the rate from the difference in inflation.
The rule
If the spot rate starts in equilibrium, any change in the inflation gap is offset over the long run by an equal and opposite change in the spot rate.
S₂ = S₁ × (1+πforeign) ÷ (1+πhome)
The intuition
  • Higher inflation at home → your exports get pricier and less competitive.
  • Imports become cheaper than domestic goods.
  • That pushes a current-account deficit — unless the currency weakens to offset it.
  • So the high-inflation currency tends to depreciate.

6.1 · Does It Actually Work?

Testing PPP in the real world

Decades of testing the absolute and relative versions — and the law of one price — reach two clear verdicts.
1
PPP holds well over the very long run, but poorly over short periods.
2
It works better for countries with high inflation and underdeveloped capital markets.
Why it fails short-term
  • Goods don't move at zero cost.
  • Many services aren't tradable.
  • Quality varies with local tastes and resources.
Takeaway: use PPP as a long-run anchor, never as a day-trading signal.

6.1 · Tracking Many Partners at Once

Real & nominal effective exchange-rate indices

Nominal effective index
A trade-weighted average of a currency's actual rates against all partners. It says nothing about "true value" — just how the rate compares to a base period.
Real effective index
Adjusts the nominal index for inflation differences. It shows how weighted-average purchasing power has shifted versus a base period.
E$ Real = E$ Nom × (C$ ÷ CFC)
100 = fair value. Above 100 → currency is overvalued (exports less competitive). Below 100 → undervalued (exports more competitive). If PPP held perfectly, every index would sit at exactly 100.

6.1 · When Rates Move

Exchange-rate pass-through

How much of a currency move actually shows up in the price a foreign buyer pays. PPP assumes 100% — reality is often less.
The Volvo example
A Volvo built in Belgium costs €50,000. At $1.00 = €1.00 it sells for $50,000 in the U.S. If the euro appreciates 20%:
Complete pass-through
$60,000
full 20% rise
Partial pass-through
$58,000
only 16% rise
Why a firm absorbs the move
  • A full price jump could crush sales volume abroad.
  • Imported parts get cheaper when the home currency strengthens, cushioning costs.
  • Existing contracts delay when changes hit prices.
FIRE 473 · International FinanceCH 6 · 6.1 PRICES & EXCHANGE RATES
6.1 · The Deciding Factor

Price elasticity sets the pass-through

ep = %ΔQ ÷ %ΔP
The percentage change in quantity demanded for each percentage change in price. It decides whether a firm dares to pass the currency move through.
Inelastic · e < 1
  • Quantity barely reacts to price.
  • Firm can pass through a lot — a higher price raises revenue.
  • High pass-through is safe.
Elastic · e > 1
  • Buyers flee when price rises.
  • A 20% rise can cut total revenue.
  • Firm absorbs the move — low pass-through.

6.2
Section Two
Interest Rates & Exchange Rates
Money has a price — the interest rate — and that price ties directly to currencies. From the Fisher effect we build forward rates, interest rate parity, and the arbitrage that keeps them all in line.
6.2 · The Price of Money

The Fisher Effect

A country's nominal interest rate is built from two parts: a real return investors demand, plus compensation for expected inflation.
i  =  r  +  π  +  (r × π) i = nominal rate  ·  r = real rate  ·  π = expected inflation  ·  the last term is usually tiny and often dropped
Real rate r
The true purchasing-power return — what your money actually earns after inflation is stripped out.
Inflation π
Lenders must be paid back in cheaper money, so they add expected inflation on top.
Nominal rate i
The rate you actually see quoted on a bond or deposit — real plus inflation combined.
Key implication: if real rates are equal across countries, then differences in nominal rates simply reflect differences in expected inflation.

6.2 · From Rates to Currencies

The International Fisher Effect

If real rates are equal everywhere, the currency of the country with the higher nominal interest rate should depreciate — by roughly the interest-rate difference.
The relationship
% change in spot ≈ iforeign − ihome
A high nominal rate signals high expected inflation. That inflation erodes the currency, so the extra interest you earn is roughly cancelled by the currency's fall.
Why it must hold
  • If a high-rate currency didn't fall, investors would pile in for the free extra yield.
  • That capital flow bids the currency up now and forces an expected decline later.
  • The result: no free lunch from chasing high interest rates alone.
Same engine as PPP: there it was inflation differences that moved the currency; here it is interest-rate differences — which embed those same inflation expectations.

6.2 · Locking in a Rate

The forward rate

A forward rate is an exchange rate agreed today for a currency trade that settles on a future date. It is not a forecast — it is set purely by the two countries' interest rates.
How it is calculated
F = S × (1 + iforeign × d/360)  ÷  (1 + ihome × d/360)
S = spot rate · d = days forward · rates are annual. The currency with the lower interest rate sells at a premium forward.
Premium vs. discount
  • Forward premium — the foreign currency buys more dollars forward than spot. Happens when its interest rate is below the dollar's.
  • Forward discount — it buys fewer. Happens when its rate is above the dollar's.
Annualized forward premium on the foreign currency:  f = (S − F) / F × (360/d) × 100  — a positive number means the market will pay more for that currency in the future.

6.2 · Worked Example

Swiss franc, 90 days forward

The setup
Spot rate (SFr per $1)1.4800
U.S. dollar interest rate8.00%
Swiss franc interest rate4.00%
Days forward90
The franc's interest rate (4%) sits below the dollar's (8%), so we expect the franc to trade at a forward premium.
Step 1 · Forward rate
1.4800 × (1 + .04×90/360) ÷ (1 + .08×90/360)
= 1.4800 × 1.0100 ÷ 1.0200 = 1.4655
Step 2 · Annualized premium
(1.4800 − 1.4655) ÷ 1.4655 × (360/90)
= +3.96% premium
Read it: it takes fewer francs to buy a dollar forward (1.4655 < 1.4800), so the franc is worth more in the future — exactly the +3.96% premium the lower interest rate implies.

6.2 · When Parity Breaks

Covered Interest Arbitrage (CIA)

When the forward rate is mispriced, a trader can borrow in one currency, invest covered in the other, and lock in a riskless profit — until the trades themselves push parity back.
The arbitrageur's loop
  • Spot the currency offering the better covered return.
  • Convert in at spot, invest at that currency's rate.
  • Sell the proceeds forward today, so the exit rate is locked.
  • Pocket the difference — no open currency risk.
Worked figures — $1,000,000, 180 days
Spot (¥ per $)106.00
Forward, 180d (¥ per $)103.50
Dollar rate8.00%
Yen rate4.00%
Even though the dollar pays a higher rate, the yen's forward premium more than makes up for it — so the covered yen route wins.

6.2 · Betting on the Spread

Uncovered carry trade

Skip the forward cover and you have the carry trade: borrow a low-rate currency, invest in a high-rate one, and hope the exchange rate doesn't move against you. The interest gap is the reward; the currency is the risk.
Covered vs. uncovered
  • Covered (CIA): lock the exit rate forward — riskless, and parity erases the profit.
  • Uncovered (carry): leave the exit rate open — you keep the full interest gap if the currency cooperates.
The hidden danger
International Fisher warns the high-rate currency should fall. If it falls far enough, the currency loss wipes out the interest gain — and leverage turns that into a disaster.
Real-world face: for two decades, Japanese retail investors — nicknamed Mrs. Watanabe — borrowed cheap yen to chase higher yields abroad. We model her trade at the end of this deck.

6.2 · Putting It Together

Where all the rates settle

Spot, forward, interest rates, and inflation are not four free numbers — they are locked together. Arbitrage drives the market toward a single equilibrium point where every parity condition holds at once.
Off equilibrium
If forward premium ≠ interest gap, covered arbitrage (CIA) profits flow until the gap closes.
The pull
Each trade nudges spot, forward, or rates back toward parity. The profit shrinks as it does.
At equilibrium
Forward premium = interest differential = expected inflation differential = expected spot change.
At the equilibrium point, a 4% interest differential, a 4% forward premium, a 4% inflation gap, and a 4% expected currency move are all the same 4% — viewed through four different windows.

6.3
Section Three
The Forward Rate as a Predictor
If markets are efficient, today's forward rate is the market's best unbiased guess of the future spot rate. Individual guesses miss — but the misses should cancel out, averaging to zero.
6.3 · A Noisy but Centered Guess

Forward = unbiased predictor

"Unbiased" does not mean accurate. Each period the actual spot lands above or below the forward — sometimes by a lot — but with no systematic tilt in either direction.
What unbiased means
  • The forward rate sits in the middle of the range of possible future spots.
  • Errors are random — overshoot and undershoot are equally likely.
  • Average the errors over many periods and they trend toward zero.
Why it follows from efficiency
If the forward consistently over-predicted, traders would bet against it and profit. Those bets would move the forward until the bias disappeared. A persistent error is a money-making signal — so it can't last.
The catch: "on average, eventually" is cold comfort on any single trade. The forward is centered, but the spread around it is exactly the currency risk a hedger pays to avoid.

6.3 · The Debate

Is the currency market efficient?

If forward rates are truly unbiased, speculation can't earn consistent profits and hedging the forward costs nothing on average. The evidence is mixed.
Points toward efficient
  • Major currencies are deeply traded with low transaction costs.
  • Information spreads almost instantly across global desks.
  • Over long horizons, forward errors do roughly average out.
Points toward inefficient
  • The carry trade earned positive returns for years — a forward-bias puzzle.
  • Currencies overshoot and trend longer than fundamentals justify.
  • Central-bank intervention can hold rates away from parity.
Working conclusion: the market is close to efficient for big currencies over long periods, but short-run biases are real enough to fund entire trading strategies — and to blow them up.

Mrs. Watanabe & the Yen Carry Trade
For two decades, ordinary Japanese investors borrowed near-zero-cost yen to chase yield around the world — turning the carry trade from a textbook idea into a global force.
Mini-Case · Background

Who is "Mrs. Watanabe"?

A nickname for Japan's legions of retail currency investors — often households managing savings — who collectively moved enough money to sway global exchange rates.
The setup
  • Japanese interest rates sat near zero for years after the 1990s.
  • Yields abroad — Australia, New Zealand, emerging markets — were far higher.
  • Borrowing yen to invest abroad captured the gap, period after period.
The mechanics
Borrow ¥ cheap → convert to a high-yield currency → earn the spread → convert back later
It works beautifully as long as the yen stays weak or falls. The danger is a sudden yen rally that erases years of gains at once.
Why it matters: this is the uncovered carry trade from Section 6.2, lived out at national scale — a direct test of whether International Fisher really makes the high-yield currency fall.

Mini-Case · Three Forces

Why Japan, why then

1️⃣
Ultra-low rates
A long fight against deflation pinned Japanese interest rates near zero, making yen the cheapest funding currency on earth.
2️⃣
Open capital flows
Japanese savers could freely move money abroad, and high-saving households had ample funds to deploy in search of yield.
3️⃣
A persistently weak yen
For long stretches the yen drifted sideways or weakened — so the feared currency loss never showed up, and the spread was pure profit.
The paradox: International Fisher says the low-rate yen should appreciate, cancelling the trade. For years it didn't — which is exactly the forward-rate-bias puzzle that makes economists question full market efficiency.

Mini-Case · The Winner

Aussie–yen: the trade that worked

Borrow yen at ~1%, invest in Australian dollars at ~4.5%, and — crucially — the Aussie dollar rose against the yen over the period. Both the interest gap and the currency move paid off.
The numbers
Borrowed¥50,000,000
Yen borrowing rate1.0%
A$ investing rate4.5%
Spot at start¥60.91 / A$
Spot at end¥83.19 / A$
Total profit
+¥20.86M
A return of roughly 41.7% on the borrowed amount.
Where the gain came from
A small slice from the 3.5% interest spread, but the bulk from the Aussie dollar strengthening from 60.91 to 83.19 yen — the opposite of what parity predicted.

Mini-Case · The Loser

Euro–rupee: when carry bites back

Same idea, opposite outcome. Borrow euros cheaply, invest in higher-yielding rupees — but the rupee weakened, and the currency loss swallowed the entire interest gain.
The numbers
Borrowed€20,000,000
Euro borrowing rate1.0%
Rupee investing rate2.5%
Spot at start₹67.40 / €
Spot at end₹68.00 / €
Period180 days
Total result
−€28,676
A small loss — the rupee's slide beat the yield pickup.
The lesson
The 1.5% interest edge over 180 days was tiny. Even a modest depreciation of the rupee (67.40 → 68.00) was enough to turn the whole trade negative. This is International Fisher quietly doing its job.
Takeaway: carry profits are real but fragile. The interest gap is small and steady; the currency move is large and unpredictable — and it decides everything.

Reference · Key Terms

Chapter 6 vocabulary

Law of One Price
Identical goods must sell for the same price everywhere once converted to a common currency.
Purchasing Power Parity
Exchange rates adjust so currencies have equal purchasing power; relative PPP links inflation gaps to rate changes.
Fisher Effect
Nominal interest rate = real rate + expected inflation.
International Fisher Effect
The currency with the higher nominal rate is expected to depreciate by the rate differential.
Forward Rate
An exchange rate set today for settlement on a future date; priced off the interest differential.
Interest Rate Parity
The forward premium equals the interest-rate differential; covered routes must tie.
Covered Interest Arbitrage
Exploiting a mispriced forward for riskless profit, using a forward to remove currency risk.
Carry Trade (Uncovered)
Borrowing a low-rate currency to invest in a high-rate one without hedging the exit rate.

Wrap-Up · The Big Picture

What Chapter 6 proved

The unifying idea
Prices, interest rates, forward rates, and spot rates are not independent. A single differential between two countries echoes through all of them — and arbitrage is the force that keeps them aligned.
The four pillars
  • PPP ties inflation gaps to currency moves.
  • Fisher ties inflation to interest rates.
  • IRP ties interest rates to forward rates.
  • Unbiased forward ties forwards to expected spots.
In a frictionless world these conditions hold exactly. In the real world they hold approximately — and the gaps, like Mrs. Watanabe's carry trade, are where both profit and risk live.
